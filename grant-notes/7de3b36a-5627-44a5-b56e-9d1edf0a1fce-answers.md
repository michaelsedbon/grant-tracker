### 🆕 1.3 Tell us the story behind your project (max 2000 chars — Annex 1)

> ⚠️ **NEW — needs your review.** Annex 1 asks for: how the idea came about, who participated, main phases, tools/methods used.

**How the idea came about:** The project emerged from a research question: what would computation look like if it depended on living organisms rather than silicon? During a Master's programme at CRI Paris (Centre de Recherches Interdisciplinaires), the observation that Marimo algae spheres naturally float when photosynthesising and sink in darkness suggested a biological mechanism for binary switching. This natural behaviour became the foundation for the entire system.

**Main phases:**
1. *Research & prototyping* — Testing photosynthetic buoyancy with single algae spheres, measuring transition times, documenting habituation effects across multiple experiments
2. *Hardware development* — Designing and fabricating custom PCBs, stepper motor controllers, LED arrays, and sensor systems. Building the physical grid of transparent tubes with robotic arms
3. *Software development* — Building the full control stack: embedded firmware, HTTP APIs, WebSocket real-time monitoring, and a web dashboard for orchestrating read-write operations
4. *Exhibition & evaluation* — Presenting the completed system in gallery contexts, collecting audience feedback, documenting biological behaviour over extended periods

**Tools and methods:** Custom electronic design (KiCad), embedded programming, 3D printing for mechanical components, web development for the control interface, biological experimentation documented through a structured experiment notebook.

**How this approach contributed to the objectives:** Each phase was conducted as an open experiment, with results feeding directly into the next iteration. The tight coupling between biological observation and engineering response ensured the system works with the organisms' constraints rather than against them.

### 1.4 Describe your project in detail

*Cryptographic Beings* is a technological proposal for a "living hard-drive" where digital information is stored in living vegetal media instead of silicon-based transistors. The project realises binary logic through photosynthetic buoyancy switching: each "living bit" is a Marimo algae sphere (*Aegagropila linnaei*). When illuminated, photosynthesis supersaturates the sphere with oxygen micro-bubbles, decreasing its density so the sphere rises and floats. In darkness the oxygen diffuses away and the sphere gradually sinks. These two states, High (1) and Low (0), enable binary storage.

The installation comprises 30 transparent tubes arranged in a grid, each containing a Marimo sphere in water. Robotic arms fitted with LED arrays control light exposure per tube, while custom sensor arrays and camera systems read the living state. A central controller orchestrates writing and reading operations through a web dashboard with real-time updates.

The system is not a fixed sculpture but a reprogrammable living machine. Its control code can be rewritten for each exhibition context, and in future exhibitions the machine will be reprogrammed to enable AI-driven interaction between human audiences and the algae systems, where a language model writes text into the living array and learns in real time how much information it can extract without destroying the organisms' photosynthetic vitality. This creates a perpetually renegotiated frontier between computational desire and ecological limit, where code and algae co-adapt over time.

## Section 2: NEB Core Values (40 points)

### 2.1 Sustainability (environmental)

**How does your project contribute to environmental sustainability?**

Cryptographic Beings directly addresses the environmental cost of computation and data storage. By substituting silicon-based transistors with living photosynthetic organisms, it proposes an alternative substrate where data permanence depends on ecological stewardship rather than energy-intensive server farms.

Each "write" cycle consumes real metabolic resources: light, nutrients, and recovery time. This makes the environmental cost of data tangible and visible, rather than hidden in distant data centres. Throughput is limited to the pace of photosynthesis, confronting viewers with the gap between silicon's near-instant switching and life's slower rhythms. Data durability depends on the health of living organisms; over-extraction through too many writes bleaches and kills the algae, permanently destroying stored data.

The core computational mechanism is photosynthesis itself, a carbon-absorbing process. And the Marimo algae are slow-growing, long-lived organisms (some specimens exceed 100 years), embodying durability and patience over planned obsolescence.

### 2.2 Inclusion

**How does your project promote social inclusion?**

The installation is built around universally observable phenomena: floating and sinking balls, light and darkness. This makes both the binary logic and the underlying biological processes immediately legible, regardless of technical background. The living organisms engage viewers across generations and across levels of knowledge. Children can directly observe biological processes in action while adults engage with the deeper philosophical implications about data, ecology, and accelerationism.

In future exhibitions, the machine will be reprogrammed to communicate AI interaction with human and algae systems, making the negotiation between artificial intelligence and biological life accessible and tangible for non-specialist audiences.

The Marimo algae themselves hold cultural significance across borders. In Japan, they are celebrated through the Marimo Matsuri, a traditional festival honouring the spherical algae as a natural treasure, connecting the project to indigenous ecological knowledge and offering a bridge between contemporary technological art and long-standing cultural relationships with non-human organisms.

### 2.3 Aesthetics & Quality of Experience

**How does your project contribute to aesthetics and quality of experience?**

Thirty transparent tubes with luminous green algae spheres create a mesmerising kinetic display, with spheres slowly rising and sinking in choreographed patterns. The pace of photosynthesis, from minutes to hours per state change, creates a contemplative, meditative viewing experience that stands in stark contrast to the immediacy of screen-based digital art.

The materials are honest and legible: water, glass, light, and living organisms. The bubbling of oxygen, the gentle movement of water, and the green glow of chlorophyll under illumination engage multiple senses simultaneously. The work operates at several layers of reading, from the immediate visual impact of a beautiful kinetic sculpture to a deeper engagement with questions of accelerationism, bio-extractive economies, and what it means to care for a technology that is also alive.

## Section 3: Working Principles (35 points)

### 3.1 Participatory process

**How were communities involved?**

Each exhibition is configured as a live experiment where audience presence directly affects the installation. The warmth of visitors' bodies and the CO2 from their breathing subtly alter the algae's metabolic rate, shifting photosynthetic activity and influencing how quickly the spheres respond to light changes. The audience is therefore not passive but metabolically entangled with the living memory system, making their presence a variable in the computation itself.

Workshops are currently in preparation to build easily accessible, frugal 3D-printed robots based on the principles of Cryptographic Beings. These workshops are designed to target children's audiences, allowing young participants to construct their own miniature bio-hybrid devices and develop hands-on understanding of biological computation, sustainability, and the relationship between technology and living systems.

The project was presented at CRI Paris (Centre de Recherches Interdisciplinaires) and London College of Communication, where students engaged with the concepts through lectures and hands-on exploration of simplified bioreactor systems.

### 3.2 Multi-level engagement

**How does your project engage at multiple levels?**

The project connects exhibition spaces where general audiences encounter the work, academic environments where it generates new research questions at the intersection of biology and computation, and educational settings where it becomes a tool for teaching sustainability and scientific thinking. Horizontally, it engages across disciplines: art, biology, computer science, philosophy, and ecology. Vertically, it moves from local maker communities to international exhibitions.

The work has been exhibited in major international art institutions and festivals, engaging general audiences with questions about technology and ecology. It has been published in specialised journals including IEEE, and presented at academic conferences, contributing to ongoing research on bio-hybrid systems. Partnerships with university research labs, particularly in synthetic biology, ensure the scientific rigour of the biological aspects.

At the community level, the planned workshop programme will extend the project's reach into education, bringing biological computation concepts to audiences who would not typically encounter them in gallery settings.

### 3.3 Transdisciplinary approach

**What fields does your project bring together?**

Cryptographic Beings sits at the intersection of several fields that rarely converge. The biological layer draws on algae ecology, photosynthesis research, and buoyancy physics. The computational layer engages with binary logic, data storage theory, and embedded systems engineering, while AI integration adds a dimension of machine learning and adaptive behaviour. The artistic and philosophical dimension draws on critical discourse around accelerationism, Infocene theory, bio-ethics, and post-human ontology. And the engineering work, from custom PCB design to IoT networking, ensures the installation functions reliably as a real system rather than a mere conceptual proposition, while the ecological framing ties everything back to questions of care-based computing and data infrastructure as ecosystem.

This is not interdisciplinary collaboration in the usual sense of experts contributing to a shared project. Rather, all of these domains are embedded within a single practice, each informing the others at every stage of development.

## Section 4: Innovation (5 points)

**What is novel about your approach?**

Cryptographic Beings is, to the best of our knowledge, the only device that stores digital data in living media. Other projects in the bio-computing space have explored data storage in DNA, but DNA is an inert molecule; it does not metabolise, grow, or respond to its environment. It is used as a passive storage substrate, essentially a molecular hard drive. Cryptographic Beings takes a fundamentally different approach: the data is stored in the active biological behaviour of living organisms, specifically in the dynamic equilibrium between photosynthesis and respiration. The "memory" is not inscribed in a molecule but enacted by a living process.

This means that data permanence is contingent on ecological health. It cannot be separated from care. This is not merely a technical novelty but a conceptual shift: computation becomes a symbiotic relationship rather than an extractive one.

The machine also serves as a platform for exploring AI-biology co-adaptation. When a language model is connected to the living array, the AI must learn to negotiate with biological constraints in real time, adjusting its behaviour based on the organisms' photosynthetic capacity. This creates a unique experimental setup where artificial intelligence encounters genuine ecological limits.

## Section 6: Replicability & Scalability (5 points)

### 🆕 Scalability (reworked per Annex 1)

> ⚠️ **NEW — needs your review.** Annex 1 asks: which elements are transferable, why it can serve as a model, skills/partnerships needed, local adaptations.

**Why this project can serve as a model:** Cryptographic Beings demonstrates a methodology for embedding computation in biological systems that is fundamentally organism-agnostic. The principles (observe a switchable biological behaviour → build sensing and actuation around it → create a read-write interface) can be applied to any organism displaying binary-like states.

**Most transferable elements:**
- The conceptual framework (living vs inert data storage, care-based computing)
- The modular hardware architecture (scalable tube grid, standard electronic components)
- The software stack (web-based control, real-time monitoring, API-driven operation)
- The workshop methodology (frugal 3D-printed kits for educational contexts)

**Skills and partnerships:** Replication benefits from basic electronics and programming knowledge, though the planned workshop kits are designed to lower this barrier significantly. Partnerships with biology labs are valuable for deeper scientific investigation but not required for educational or artistic deployments.

**Local adaptation:** The concept can be adapted to local organisms and cultural contexts. Any species displaying a switchable behaviour (photosynthetic, chemotactic, gravitropic) could serve as a "living bit." Local biological knowledge and ecological traditions can inform the choice of organism, creating culturally specific versions of the project.

A single-tube demonstration version can be built for under €50, making it suitable for classroom settings. The full installation scales from small configurations to 30 or more tubes depending on exhibition space.
